# Background

## Every engineering project has an origin

Every engineering project begins with a technical challenge.

CHOMS Platform did not.

It began with frustration.

Not frustration caused by technology itself, but by the realization that professional experience, continuous learning and engineering capability are not always visible through a traditional résumé.

For several years I remained outside the professional software industry.

From the perspective of a recruiter, that period could easily be interpreted as technical stagnation.

From my own perspective, however, the opposite was true.

Technology never disappeared from my daily life.

Programming, infrastructure, software architecture, electronics, distributed systems and continuous learning remained constant interests.

The problem was never a lack of knowledge.

The problem was demonstrating that knowledge.

Eventually I reached a simple conclusion.

Instead of explaining my engineering capability, I would build something capable of explaining it for me.

That decision became the foundation of CHOMS Platform.

---

## Looking backwards

Throughout my professional career I have had the opportunity to participate in projects of very different sizes.

Some were technically challenging.

Others were organisationally complex.

Some were well managed.

Others became examples of how engineering projects should never be executed.

Those experiences shaped the way I think today.

They taught me that successful technology projects are rarely successful because of technology alone.

They succeed because of engineering discipline.

Good documentation.

Clear architecture.

Well-defined responsibilities.

Controlled change management.

Long-term thinking.

And above all, understanding the problem before attempting to solve it.

One lesson became particularly important.

Writing code is often the easiest part of software engineering.

Designing systems that remain understandable, maintainable and scalable years later is considerably more difficult.

That lesson would later become one of the pillars of CHOMS Platform.

---

## Lessons learned from previous projects

During previous positions I experienced both excellent engineering practices and poor ones.

Some organisations invested significant effort in documentation, analysis and governance before implementation began.

Others followed the opposite approach.

Development started immediately, while requirements, architecture and documentation were left for later.

Those projects usually paid the price.

Over time I discovered that most technical problems were not actually technical.

They were communication problems.

Documentation problems.

Planning problems.

Decision-making problems.

Architecture problems.

As a consequence, I gradually changed the way I approached engineering.

Today I strongly believe that implementation should never be the first phase of a project.

Understanding must always come first.

Architecture follows.

Implementation comes afterwards.

Documentation accompanies every step.

This philosophy is deeply embedded throughout CHOMS Platform.

Every significant decision is expected to leave a documented trace explaining not only what changed, but why that decision was taken.

Future contributors—including my future self—should never have to guess the reasoning behind an architectural choice.

The documentation exists to preserve that knowledge.

---

## Building something worth maintaining

At some point another important idea emerged.

If I was going to invest hundreds or even thousands of hours building an engineering platform, I did not want to create another demonstration project.

I wanted to build something that would still be useful many years later.

That simple objective changed everything.

The project stopped being focused on learning individual technologies.

Instead, it became focused on building a sustainable engineering ecosystem.

A platform capable of evolving continuously without losing coherence.

A platform where infrastructure, software, governance, operations and documentation evolve together instead of independently.

That vision transformed what initially looked like a small homelab into a long-term engineering program.

The infrastructure became only one component of a much larger objective.

The real product was never the servers.

The real product was the engineering process itself.

---

## The beginning of a different journey

CHOMS Platform is therefore much more than a collection of services running on inexpensive hardware.

It represents a personal commitment to engineering excellence.

Every phase of the project will continue to document successes, failures, architectural changes, technology evaluations and strategic decisions.

Nothing is considered too small to document if it contributes to understanding how the platform evolved.

Years from now I expect to look back at these documents and see much more than technical notes.

I expect to see the complete engineering journey.

Not only of the platform.

But also of the engineer who built it.
